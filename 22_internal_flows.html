Internal and External flows

Internal flows

  • Flow in a conduit (completely filled with the fluid)
  • Flow driven primarily by pressure difference
  • Pressure drop and flow rate given pipe geometry and components
  • Understand laminar and turbulent flow in pipes
  • Calculate major and minor losses associated with pipe flow

Laminar and turbulent flow

  • Laminar: smooth streamlines; highly ordered motion
  • Turbulent: velocity fluctuations; highly disordered motion
  • Transition: from laminar to turbulent

Reynolds number

Flow regime depends on the ratio of inertial forces to viscous forces

\[\boxed{\text{Re} = \dfrac{V D}{\nu} = \dfrac{\rho V D}{\mu}}\]

Characteristic length

Understanding Laminar and Turbulent Flow

Reynolds number is dimensionless

\[\text{Re} = \dfrac{\textcolor{mediumpurple}{\text{Inertial forces}}}{\textcolor{orangered}{\text{Viscous forces}}}\]

  • Large \(\text{Re}\) → Inertial forces >> Viscous forces
  • Small \(\text{Re}\) → Viscous forces >> Inertial forces
  • Critical Reynolds number

Hydraulic diameter

For flow in noncircular pipes \(\text{Re}\) is calculated with \(D_h\) \[D_h = \dfrac{4 A}{p}\]

Critical Reynolds values

Transition from laminar to turbulent also depends on surface roughness, pipe vibrations, fluctuations in the upstream flow

\(\text{Re} \leq 2300\) Laminar flow
\(2300 \leq \text{Re} \leq 4000\) Transitional flow
\(\text{Re} \geq 4000\) Turbulent flow

Transitional flow

Entrance region

  • Boundary layer: viscous effects dominate reducing velocity
  • Velocity at the pipe midsection increases to keep \(\dot{m}\) constant
  • Boundary layers merge and inviscid core region disappears
  • Fully developed flow
  • Velocity profile becomes constant
  • Hydrodynamic entry length \(L_e\)

Developing velocity profile

Shear stress

Entrance lengths

From dimensional analysis \[L_e = f(D,V,\rho,\mu) \Rightarrow \dfrac{L_e}{D} = g \left( \dfrac{\rho V D}{\mu} \right) = g (\text{Re})\]

For laminar flow \(\dfrac{L_e}{D} \approx 0.06 \text{Re}\)

For turbulent flow \(\dfrac{L_e}{D} \approx 1.6 (\text{Re})^{\frac{1}{4}}\)

Example

A 0.5-in-diameter water pipe is 60 ft long and delivers water at 5 gal/min at 20°C (\(\nu=10^{-5}\,\mathrm{ft^2/s}\)). What fraction of this pipe is taken up by the entrance region?

Solution

\[Q = (5\,\mathrm{gal/min})(0.00223\,\mathrm{ft^3/s}) = 0.0111\,\mathrm{ft^3/s}\]

\[V = \dfrac{Q}{A} = \dfrac{0.0111}{(\pi/4)(0.5)(1/12)} = 8.17\,\mathrm{ft/s}\]

\[\text{Re} = \dfrac{V D}{\nu} = \dfrac{(8.17)(0.5)(1/12)}{10^{-5}} = 34041.7\]

Turbulent flow

\[\dfrac{L_e}{D} = 1.6 (\text{Re})^{1/4} = (1.6)(34041.7)^{1/4} = 21.73\]

\[\dfrac{L_e}{L} = \dfrac{21.73(0.5)(1/12)}{60} = 0.015\] or 1.5%
