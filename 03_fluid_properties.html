Dimensions and units

  • Dimensions are used to quantify physical variables
  • A unit is a way to assign a magnitude to each dimension
  • The fundamental dimensions include:
    • Mass \(\{M\}\)
    • Length \(\{L\}\)
    • Time \(\{T\}\)
    • Temperature \(\{\Theta\}\)

  • All other variables can be expressed in terms of those dimensions

    \[F = m a\] \[\{F\} = \{MLT^{-2}\}\]

Unit systems

  • International System of Units (SI)
  • British Gravitational System (BG)

  • Units are defined in terms of the basic units

Dimensional homogeneity

Let's use the example of the Bernoulli equation \[p_0 = p + \dfrac{1}{2} \rho V^2 + \rho g Z\] where \(p\) are pressures, \(V\) is velocity, \(\rho\) is density, \(g\) is gravity acceleration, and \(Z\) is altitude.

\[\text{LHS:}~\{M L T^{-2} L^{-2}\} = \{M L^{-1} T^{-2}\}\] \[\text{RHS:}~\{M L^{-1} T^{-2}\} + \{M L^{-3}\} \{L^2 T^{-2}\}\] \[+ \{M L^{-3}\} \{L T^{-2}\} \{L\} =\{M L^{-1} T^{-2}\}\]

Example

A body weighs \(1000\mathrm{lbf}\) at earth gravity \(g=32.174\mathrm{ft/s^2}\). What will the weight of this body be in N if it is exposed to the moonâ€™s standard acceleration \(g_m = 1.62\mathrm{m/s^2}\)

\[F=W=mg\] \[\Rightarrow m=\dfrac{W}{g} = \dfrac{1000\mathrm{lbf}}{32.174\mathrm{ft/s^2}}=31.08\mathrm{slugs}\] \[m = 31.08\mathrm{slugs} = (31.08\mathrm{slugs})(14.5939\mathrm{kg/slug}) = 454\mathrm{kg}\] \[F = W_m = m g_{m} = (454\mathrm{kg})(1.62\mathrm{m/s^2}) = 735\mathrm{N}\]

Homogeneous versus Dimensionally Inconsistent Equations

  • All theoretical equations are dimensionally homogeneous
  • Many empirical equations can be dimensionally inconsistent
  • Example of volume flow rate through partially open valve

\[Q = C_v \left( \dfrac{\Delta p}{SG} \right)^{1/2}\]

\[\{L^3 T^{-1}\} \neq \{M^{1/2} L^{-1/2} T\}\]

\(C_v\) is not dimensionless, but has \(\{L^{7/2} M^{-1/2}\}\)

\[C_v \sim \text{pipe size}^2\]

\[Q = C_d A_{\text{opening}} \left( \dfrac{\Delta p}{\rho} \right)^{1/2}\]

Manning equation

In 1890 Robert Manning proposed the following empirical formula for the average velocity \(V\) in uniform flow due to gravity down an open channel \[V = \dfrac{1.49}{n} R^{2/3} S^{1/2}\]

  • \(n\) is roughness factor (dimensionless)
  • \(S\) is slope (tangent of angle between bottom and horizontal)
  • \(R\) is hydraulic radius (flow area divided by wetted perimeter)

Quiz!

Solution

\[V=\dfrac{1.49}{n} R^{2/3} S^{1/2}\] \[\Rightarrow \{ L T^{-1} \} = \{1.49\} \{1\} \{L^{2/3}\} \{1\}\] \[\{1.49\} = \{L^{1/3} T^{-1}\}\] Converting to SI \[(1.49\mathrm{ft^{1/3}/s})(0.3048\mathrm{m/ft})^{1/3} = 1.00\mathrm{m^{1/3}/s}\] \[V = \dfrac{1}{n} R^{2/3} S^{1/2}\]

Thermodynamic Properties of a Fluid

  • Pressure \(p\)
  • Density \(\rho\)
  • Temperature \(T\)
  • Internal energy \(\hat{u}\)
  • Enthalpy \(h = \hat{u} + p/\rho\)
  • Entropy \(s\)
  • Specific heat \(c_p\) and \(c_v\)
  • Coefficient of viscosity \(\mu\)
  • Thermal conductivity \(k\)

Pressure

  • Defined as stress at a point in a static fluid
  • One of the most dynamic variable in fluid mechanics
  • Gradients in pressure often drive a fluid flow

Density and specific weight/gravity

\[\text{Density}~~ \rho = \dfrac{m}{v}\] \[\text{Specific weight}~~ \gamma = \rho g\]

  • Density in liquids is nearly constant
  • Increases by 1% if pressure increases by $×$220
  • Heaviest common liquid is mercury and the lightest gas is hydrogen
  • Specific weight is useful in hydrostatic pressure applications

Potential and Kinetic energies

  • Total energy stored per unit mass of fluid
  • \(e = \hat{u} + \dfrac{1}{2} V^2 + (- \mathbf{g} \cdot \mathbf{r})\)
  • Internal + Kinetic + Potential

State relations for gases

  • Assumption of single-phase substances (e.g., liquid water, air)
  • For gases at high temperatures and low pressures

Perfect-gas Law \[p = \rho R T\] \[R = c_p - c_v\]

For air, \(R=287\,\mathrm{m^2/s^2 \cdot K}\) and \(\rho = 1.22\,\mathrm{kg/m^3}\)

Specific heats

  • How much heat is added per unit temperature rise

  • The following approximation should work in air flow analysis

    \[k_{\text{air}} = \dfrac{c_p}{c_v} \approx 1.4\] \[c_v = \dfrac{R}{k-1} \approx 718\,\mathrm{m^2/(s^2 \cdot K)}\] \[c_p = \dfrac{kR}{k-1} \approx 1005\,\mathrm{m^2/(s^2 \cdot K)}\]

State relations for liquids

  • Density and heat capacities are constant
  • \(\rho_{\text{water}}=998\,\mathrm{kg/m^3}\)
  • \(c_p \approx c_v = 4210\,\mathrm{m^2/(s^2 \cdot K)}\)
  • Empirical pressure-density relation \[\dfrac{p}{p_a} \approx (B+1)\left( \dfrac{\rho}{\rho_a}\right)^n - B\]
  • \(B \approx 3000\) and \(n \approx 7\)

Example

The pressure at the deepest part of the ocean is approximately 1100 atm. Estimate the density of seawater at this pressure, if surface density is 1020 kg/m3.

\[\dfrac{p}{p_a} = (3000+1)\left( \dfrac{\rho}{\rho_a}\right)^7 - 3000\] \[1100 = (3001)\left(\dfrac{\rho}{1020}\right)^7 - 3000\] \[\dfrac{\rho}{1020} = \left( \dfrac{4100}{3001} \right)^{1/7} = 1.046\] \[\rho = 1066.9\,\mathrm{kg/m^3}\]
